# Scene packages

All viewer-facing copy below is English. Only the intro has a new image prompt. The ten scene images have been converted to `.png` without changing their visual content.

## Intro — `00-intro.png`

- Title: The Dodo
- Subtitle: *Raphus cucullatus*
- Hook: A bird that vanished after people arrived
- TTS: “On a remote island in the Indian Ocean, a bird evolved without fear of people. Then ships arrived. Within a few generations, the dodo had disappeared. Its story is not about a foolish animal. It is about how quickly an isolated world can change.”
- Visual: use the sole prompt in `04-image-prompts.txt`.

## 01 — `01-opening.png`

- Title / subtitle: A Bird Without Fear / Mauritius, before human settlement
- TTS: “Before people settled Mauritius, the dodo lived on an island with no native terrestrial mammal predators. Its ancestors had arrived long before and gradually lost the need for flight. The result was a large ground-dwelling pigeon, shaped by isolation rather than by danger from humans.”
- Fact note: The dodo was endemic to Mauritius: it lived nowhere else on Earth.
- Motion / focus: zoom-to-focus; X 0.48, Y 0.50. Title 0.5–4.7s; subtitle 0.9–4.5s.

## 02 — `02-habitat.png`

- Title / subtitle: An Island Apart / The Mascarene Islands
- TTS: “Mauritius lies east of Madagascar in the Indian Ocean. Volcanic and isolated, it became home to a distinctive community of plants and animals. The dodo belonged to the pigeon family, but on this island its lineage took a remarkable path: bigger body, smaller wings, and a life spent on the forest floor.”
- Fact note: Genetic studies place the dodo among pigeons and doves; the Nicobar pigeon is a close living relative.
- Motion / focus: pan-left-to-right; X 0.50, Y 0.47. Title 0.5–4.7s; subtitle 0.9–4.5s.

## 03 — `03-anatomy.png`

- Title / subtitle: Not a Cartoon / Reconstructing the real dodo
- TTS: “Paintings made in Europe helped create the image of a round, helpless bird. Some may have shown captive dodos that were unusually fat. Bones tell a more careful story. The living animal was a robust, mobile forest bird. Its exact appearance varied, and no complete preserved specimen survives.”
- Fact note: Reconstructions use bones, early accounts, art and living pigeons.
- Motion / focus: slow-zoom-out; X 0.50, Y 0.49. Title 0.5–4.7s; subtitle 0.9–4.5s.

## 04 — `04-behavior.png`

- Title / subtitle: Life on the Forest Floor / What the evidence suggests
- TTS: “The dodo's heavy bill and gizzard stones point to a diet that included hard foods. Historical reports and its anatomy suggest it fed from the ground on fruits, seeds and other available plant material. But the fine details of its diet remain incomplete, because scientists must reconstruct its life from fragmentary evidence.”
- Fact note: The tambalacoque-only-with-the-dodo story is not supported by sufficient evidence.
- Motion / focus: zoom-to-focus; X 0.42, Y 0.55. Title 0.5–4.7s; subtitle 0.9–4.5s.

## 05 — `05-ecosystem.png`

- Title / subtitle: A Lost Ecosystem / More than one species disappeared
- TTS: “The dodo was part of a larger web of island life. Mauritius once had giant tortoises and other large fruit-eating animals alongside it. Their loss changed how seeds moved through the forest. Today, researchers can measure many plant-animal relationships that disappeared after colonisation, even when the dodo's precise role in each one remains uncertain.”
- Fact note: Research has documented extensive losses of fruit-feeding interactions in Mauritius.
- Motion / focus: gentle-diagonal; X 0.50, Y 0.52. Title 0.5–4.7s; subtitle 0.9–4.5s.

## 06 — `06-human-arrival.png`

- Title / subtitle: The Ships Arrive / 1598
- TTS: “Dutch sailors reached Mauritius in 1598. The island later became a stop on routes across the Indian Ocean, and settlement changed the landscape. Dodos were killed by people, but hunting was only one pressure. The much bigger danger came with the animals and disturbances that travel brought ashore.”
- Fact note: The first recorded Dutch landing on Mauritius was in 1598.
- Motion / focus: pan-right-to-left; X 0.56, Y 0.50. Title 0.5–4.7s; subtitle 0.9–4.5s.

## 07 — `07-decline.png`

- Title / subtitle: The Pressures Multiply / Introduced animals and habitat loss
- TTS: “Rats, pigs, macaques and other introduced animals altered Mauritius rapidly. They could raid nests, compete for food, or damage plants. Forest clearance added another blow. No single cause explains the dodo's disappearance on its own. A small island bird faced several new pressures at the same time.”
- Fact note: Introduced species and habitat destruction are major drivers of historic Mauritian extinctions.
- Motion / focus: pan-top-to-bottom; X 0.50, Y 0.45. Title 0.5–4.7s; subtitle 0.9–4.5s.

## 08 — `08-last-years.png`

- Title / subtitle: When the Record Falls Silent / Last confirmed record: 1662
- TTS: “The last widely accepted sighting comes from 1662, on an islet off Mauritius. That does not prove the final bird died that year. A statistical study estimated that the species may have survived unseen until about 1690. What is certain is that the dodo vanished during the seventeenth century, soon after colonisation began.”
- Fact note: 1662 is the last confirmed sighting; around 1690 is a model estimate, not a witnessed death date.
- Motion / focus: slow-zoom-in; X 0.46, Y 0.52. Title 0.5–4.7s; subtitle 0.9–4.5s.

## 09 — `09-evidence.png`

- Title / subtitle: Bones in the Swamp / Mare aux Songes
- TTS: “For years, the dodo's strange early descriptions made some people doubt it had been real. Then excavations at Mare aux Songes in 1865 uncovered abundant remains. The bones turned a half-remembered bird into a scientific subject. They are still helping researchers test old stories against physical evidence.”
- Fact note: Major dodo bone discoveries at Mare aux Songes began in 1865.
- Motion / focus: zoom-to-center; X 0.50, Y 0.52. Title 0.5–4.7s; subtitle 0.9–4.5s.

## 10 — `10-legacy.png`

- Title / subtitle: The Warning Still Lives / Conservation on Mauritius
- TTS: “The dodo became a global symbol of extinction, but Mauritius also shows that decline is not always final. Conservation has helped save several surviving island species. The lesson is not that wildlife is doomed. It is that prevention, habitat protection and control of invasive species must come before a species becomes a memory.”
- Fact note: Mauritian projects continue to protect native habitat and threatened endemic species.
- Motion / focus: slow-zoom-out; X 0.50, Y 0.48. Title 0.5–4.7s; subtitle 0.9–4.5s.

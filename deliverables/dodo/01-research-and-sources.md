# Dodo research summary

## Editorially safe facts

- The dodo, *Raphus cucullatus*, was a large flightless bird endemic to Mauritius in the Indian Ocean. It belongs within the pigeon-and-dove family, Columbidae.
- Dutch sailors first recorded landing on Mauritius in 1598. Subsequent settlement, hunting, habitat clearance and introduced animals transformed the island rapidly.
- The last widely accepted record of a living dodo is from 1662, reported from an islet off Mauritius. This is a last confirmed observation, not a witnessed final death.
- Roberts and Solow (2003) estimated a likely extinction date around 1690 from the sighting record. It is best presented as a modelled estimate; the species' exact final date is unknown.
- Dodo remains from Mare aux Songes, with major finds beginning in 1865, are central evidence for modern reconstruction.
- The familiar image of a very fat and clumsy dodo is not a secure guide to its normal appearance. Modern work combines bone evidence, historical art and written accounts, while acknowledging uncertainty.
- The extinction was multi-causal. Direct killing, introduced mammals and other animals, and habitat transformation all plausibly contributed. Do not attribute it solely to hunting or to any one introduced species.
- The dodo probably participated in Mauritian forest food webs, but claims that a particular tree depended exclusively on the dodo for seed dispersal are not established. The tambalacoque-only-with-the-dodo story has been challenged.

## Claims deliberately avoided

- “The last dodo died in 1662.” The year is the last widely accepted sighting, not a confirmed death date.
- “The dodo laid one egg every year.” The historical evidence is too thin for this as a definite narration claim.
- “The dodo alone saved or dispersed tambalacoque trees.” There is insufficient evidence.
- A precise universal weight, plumage colour or running speed. These are reconstructed with uncertainty.

## Sources

1. Roberts, D. L. & Solow, A. R. (2003). “When did the dodo become extinct?” *Nature* 426, 245. DOI: [10.1038/426245a](https://doi.org/10.1038/426245a). Establishes the distinction between the 1662 record and a modelled extinction date around 1690.
2. Angst, D. et al. (2017). “Bone histology sheds new light on the ecology of the dodo.” *Scientific Reports* 7, 7993. DOI: [10.1038/s41598-017-08536-3](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-017-08536-3). Peer-reviewed analysis of dodo bone histology and seasonal ecology.
3. Young, M. T. et al. (2024). “Systematics and nomenclature of the Dodo and the Solitaire.” *Zoological Journal of the Linnean Society*. DOI: [10.1093/zoolinnean/zlae086](https://doi.org/10.1093/zoolinnean/zlae086). Current review of taxonomy and historical misconceptions.
4. Natural History Museum, London. [Extinctions on the island of the dodo are pushing plants towards extinction](https://www.nhm.ac.uk/discover/news/2023/march/extinctions-island-dodo-pushing-plants-towards-extinction.html). Summarises current research on lost Mauritian seed-dispersal interactions and cautions against the dodo–tambalacoque claim.
5. Heinen, J. et al. (2023). “Disentangling the effects of extinction and invasion on the structure of a plant–frugivore interaction network.” *Nature Communications*. Linked from the NHM summary above; supports ecosystem-context framing.
6. BirdLife International / IUCN Red List. The dodo is conventionally treated as Extinct; use the current Red List and BirdLife Data Zone for status-context checks at publication time. The package avoids claiming an IUCN-assigned exact extinction year.
7. Smithsonian Libraries and Archives, Storrs L. Olson. *The Biohistory of the Mascarenes*. Historical and specimen context for dodo research: [Smithsonian repository](https://repository.si.edu/bitstreams/1d792e0c-8d1e-4f10-a45f-a085a1fee0e8/download).

## Research note

The script and JSON use “last widely accepted sighting” for 1662 and “may have survived unseen until about 1690” for the statistical estimate. This keeps the narration accurate when sources use different conventions for “extinction date.”

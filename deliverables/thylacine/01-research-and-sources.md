# Thylacine research summary

Research checked: 23 July 2026

## Editorially safe facts

- The accepted name is *Thylacinus cynocephalus* (Harris, 1808). It was a meat-eating marsupial in the order Dasyuromorphia and the family Thylacinidae. It was the only member of that family to survive into modern times.
- Fossils and Aboriginal rock art show that thylacines once lived across mainland Australia and New Guinea as well as Tasmania.
- A 2018 study of reliable fossil dates estimated that thylacines disappeared from mainland Australia about 3,200 years ago. The cause is unresolved. Dingoes, changes in human hunting and land use, and climate have all been proposed and may have acted together.
- In Tasmania, thylacines favoured a patchwork of dry forest, wetland and grassland. Their historical range overlapped with land later used for farming.
- They had a long, lean body, a stiff tail that flowed from the spine, a narrow head, rounded upright ears, and usually 13–20 dark stripes over the rear body and tail base. A Tasmanian Government source gives a maximum total length near 1.8 metres and shoulder height near 58 centimetres. A 2020 specimen study estimated a much lower average adult mass, about 16.7 kilograms, than the older figure of 29.5 kilograms.
- Much of their behaviour is reconstructed from captive animals, old reports and anatomy. They were mainly active at night or around dusk, usually avoided people, and probably hunted alone or in pairs. Reported prey included wallabies, smaller mammals and birds. Claims that they regularly caused major sheep losses were likely overstated.
- Private rewards for killing thylacines began by 1830. The Tasmanian Government bounty ran from 1888 to 1909 and paid £1 for an adult and 10 shillings for a young animal. Official sources report more than 2,180 payments; the detailed Tasmanian timeline records 2,184.
- Direct killing was the clearest driver of the Tasmanian decline. Habitat change, reduced prey and competition with dogs may also have added pressure. Disease has been proposed, but a peer-reviewed population model found that known human pressures could explain the extinction without requiring an epidemic.
- The last known shooting of a wild thylacine was in 1930. Current museum research identifies the last known individual as an old female caught by trapper Elias Churchill in the Florentine Valley and sold to Hobart Zoo in May 1936.
- The last known thylacine died at Hobart Zoo during the night of 7 September 1936. Legal protection had begun only 59 days earlier.
- Later sightings and searches have produced no conclusive evidence of survival. The species is listed as Extinct under Australia’s EPBC Act and extinct under Tasmanian law. It is also treated as Extinct by the IUCN.
- Australia marks 7 September as National Threatened Species Day, using the thylacine’s loss as a warning for species that can still be protected.

## Critical uncertainties and disputed points

- **The last animal:** Many older sources say the final zoo animal was captured in 1933 and call it “Benjamin,” a male. Paddle and Medlock’s 2023 archival study identifies a later arrival: an old female caught in 1936. The package uses this newer finding and does not use the name Benjamin.
- **The last wild record:** The last known wild shooting was in 1930, but the final known animal was itself caught from the wild in 1936. The narration keeps these two facts separate.
- **Mainland disappearance:** Reliable fossil dates support an extinction estimate near 3,200 years ago. A 2026 rock-art study raises the possibility of later survival in northern Australia, but artwork cannot by itself date the last living animal. The script therefore says “the fossil record places the loss at about 3,200 years ago.”
- **Cause on the mainland:** Dingoes are not presented as the sole cause. Human activity and climate remain competing or overlapping explanations.
- **Cause in Tasmania:** Hunting and bounty pressure are well documented. Habitat loss, prey decline, dogs and disease are presented as possible added pressures, not equally proven causes.
- **Body size:** Older references often repeat an average near 29.5 kilograms. A 2020 three-dimensional study of 93 adults estimated a mixed-sex mean of 16.7 kilograms. The visual prompts use a lean, medium-sized animal and avoid a heavy wolf-like build.
- **Behaviour and diet:** No modern field study exists. Claims about pack hunting, very large prey or a precise hunting method remain uncertain. The narration uses “likely,” “reports suggest” and “may.”

## Editorial decision

The story focuses on the evidence that is strongest: a distinct marsupial predator, a documented campaign of persecution, legal protection that came 59 days before the last known death, and a verified record that ends in 1936. The script does not claim that disease caused the extinction, that dingoes alone erased mainland populations, or that any modern sighting has been confirmed.
